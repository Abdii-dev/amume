MALARIA

Malaria is a mosquito-borne disease caused by the Plasmodium parasite. It is a life-threatening disease caused by parasites that are spread to people through the bites of infected female Anopheles mosquitoes. There are 5 parasite species that cause malaria in humans, and 2 of these species – P. Falciparum that can severe illness and death and P. Vivax – pose the greatest threat, P. Ovale, and P. Malariae generally cause a milder form of malaria. Closely half of the world's population was at risk of malaria in 2021. Some population groups are at considerably higher risk of contracting malaria and developing severe disease: children under 5 years of age, infants, pregnant women and patients with HIV/AIDS, as well as people with low immunity moving to areas with intense malaria transmission such as migrant workers, travellers and mobile populations.

The disease is the consequence of infection by a protozoan parasite from the genus Plasmodium with most morbidity and mortality caused by P. Falciparum. Global concern despite substantial reduction in incidence over the past twenty years. Public health interventions to increase the uptake of preventive measures have contributed to this decline but their impact has not been uniform.

Malaria imposes substantial costs to both governments and individuals. Costs to governments include maintenance, supply and staffing of health facilities; purchase of drugs and supplies; public health interventions against malaria, such as insecticide spraying or distribution of insecticide-treated bed nets; lost days of work with resulting loss of income; and lost opportunities for joint economic ventures and tourism. Costs to individuals and their families include purchase of drugs for treating malaria at home; expenses for travel to, and treatment at, clinics and dispensaries; lost days of work; absence from school; expenses for preventive measures; and expenses for burial in case of deaths. it occurs mostly in poor tropical and subtropical areas of the world. In many of the countries affected by malaria, it is a leading cause of illness and death. In areas with high transmission, the most vulnerable groups are young children, and pregnant women. The costs of malaria – to individuals, families, communities, nations – are enormous.

Vulnerability of children to malaria

Under-five year children are the most vulnerable group affected by malaria, they are high percent of all malaria deaths worldwide. According to center for disease control and prevention, in regions where malaria is prevalent Children have a higher risk of mortality than most adults due to their immunity. Certain groups of people are at higher risk for contracting malaria and developing severe symptoms: children under five years of age, infants, pregnant women whose immunity is decreased by pregnancy, people living with HIV/AIDS, non-immune migrants, mobile populations, and travelers. Children under five are by far the most vulnerable group as they accounted malaria deaths worldwide in 2019. Children are susceptible to severe malarial infection because they are too young to have developed partial immunity to the illness while Adults who have lived in malaria-endemic regions for most of their lives develop some protection against infection from years of exposure. Children are too young to have developed this type of immunity in their short lifespans, as their immune systems are still developing so reactions to the illness can be very severe and lead to death.Similarly, an area where there is low and unstable malaria transmission, and people have law or no immunity are more commonly affected by the disease.
